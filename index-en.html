Vesicular Trafficking: Orchestrating Cellular Organization

Eukaryotic cells contain specialized organelles. To function properly, proteins must reach the right compartment at the right time.

Proteins synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum are selected, packaged and transported to different cellular destinations, including the Golgi apparatus, plasma membrane, endosomes, vacuoles and chloroplasts. This process depends on vesicles, coat proteins such as COPII, small GTPases, adaptors, recycling complexes and membrane-fusion machinery.

Importance

An essential process for cellular life

🏅 Nobel recognition

In 2013, the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to James E. Rothman, Randy W. Schekman and Thomas C. Südhof for their discoveries of the molecular machinery that regulates vesicular transport, a process essential for protein trafficking, cellular secretion, membrane recycling and communication between organelles.

Plant biotechnology and experimental models

We use complementary plant models to study intracellular organization, compartmentalization and protein trafficking.

Physcomitrium patens

This moss belongs to the bryophyte lineage, one of the earliest groups of plants to colonize the terrestrial environment. Its dominant haploid phase allows the immediate observation of mutant phenotypes, and its high efficiency of homologous recombination makes it a powerful system for targeted genetics and precise genome editing using CRISPR/Cas9.

In the laboratory, we use this model to generate knockout, knock-in and fluorescent reporter lines associated with vesicular trafficking, endosomal recycling and protein targeting.

Nicotiana benthamiana

Nicotiana benthamiana is one of the most robust platforms for transient expression mediated by agroinfiltration with Agrobacterium tumefaciens. It allows the rapid introduction of genetic constructs into adult leaves and the expression of heterologous proteins within a few days.

We use this system for subcellular localization, protein–protein interaction assays, functional analysis of mutated variants and rapid validation of candidate cargo proteins.

Research Areas

A central question connects our research: how does the cell select, move and recycle proteins to maintain its organization and respond to the environment?

Trafficking to chloroplasts

Alternative protein-targeting routes, cargo receptors, COPII components and the evolution of mechanisms associated with chloroplast biogenesis.

Endosomal recycling

Retromer, Retriever and associated proteins involved in recycling from endosomes to the trans-Golgi network or the plasma membrane.

Vesicle biogenesis

COPII vesicle formation, cargo selection and intracellular transport in plant and eukaryotic systems.
